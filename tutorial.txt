# SMART HOTSPOT CONTROLLER - TUTORIAL & TECHNICAL GUIDE

==================================================
PART 1: HOW TO USE (STEP-BY-STEP TUTORIAL)
==================================================

STEP 1: PREPARE YOUR ANDROID PHONE (HOST)
--------------------------------------------------
Your computer couldn't find your Android device when running the flutter application. This generally means USB debugging is not enabled.
To fix this:
1. Go to your phone's Settings > About Phone.
2. Tap "Build Number" 7 times rapidly until it says "You are a developer!".
3. Go back to Settings > System (or Additional Settings) > Developer Options.
4. Turn ON "USB Debugging".
5. Connect your phone to your computer via USB. A prompt will appear on your phone asking "Allow USB Debugging?" — check the box and tap "Allow".
6. Now run `flutter run --release` again in your terminal. It will detect your phone and launch the app!

STEP 2: START THE HOTSPOT
--------------------------------------------------
1. On your Host Android phone, turn on your standard "Mobile Hotspot" feature.
2. Open the newly installed Smart Hotspot App on your active screen.
3. Tap the "Start Proxy Server" button.

STEP 3: CONFIGURE THE CLIENT
--------------------------------------------------
1. Take a second device (a laptop, an iPad, or another phone) and connect it to your Android's Wi-Fi Hotspot.
2. Go to the advanced Wi-Fi settings for that specific network connection on the Client device.
3. Look for the "Proxy" setting and change it from "None" to "Manual".
4. Enter the Hostname/IP: 192.168.43.1 (This is the standard Android hotspot local IP. If it times out, verify your gateway IP).
5. Enter the Port: 8080.
6. Save the settings.

STEP 4: MONITOR & CONTROL
--------------------------------------------------
1. Use the internet on the Client device (e.g., watch a YouTube video).
2. The Live Graph on your Host Android phone will instantly spike in real-time, showing bytes moving over the network.
3. At the bottom under "Connected Clients", you will see the device IP listed. 
4. Tap "Block", "Throttle (1mbps)", or "Set 50MB Limit" and watch the effect be dynamically enforced!


==================================================
PART 2: TECHNICAL DETAILS & ARCHITECTURE
==================================================

Why a Proxy Server Instead of Native VPNService?
--------------------------------------------------
Android OS has severe security constraints (especially Android 10+). If an app uses native `VpnService`, it intercepts ONLY traffic generated by the device itself locally. It natively blocks apps from intercepting external Wi-Fi tethering/hotspot traffic passing through the kernel gateway, unless the phone is rooted and you manipulate underlying Linux `iptables`.

To solve this completely non-root, we engineered an HTTP/TCP "Forwarding" Proxy Server in pure Dart. By mapping connected clients manually to this internal proxy port, we force all their packets recursively through our Flutter application layer before they exit to the real cell-tower internet.

How Limit Throttling Actually Works
--------------------------------------------------
When a client requests a video, the server streams bytes continuously through socket pipes. Our custom throttling logic intercepts this pipe in `_pipeThrottled`.
If you set a limit to 1 Mbps (which translates to roughly 125,000 bytes per second), our engine watches a chunk of bytes passing through. It calculates exactly how many milliseconds it should take to transmit that chunk under the restricted bandwidth limit. We use Dart's `Future.delayed()` to temporarily stall the socket writer. This acts as a robust "token bucket", forcing the data load line to slow down proportionally without severely breaking or dropping the TCP connection!

How Deep Packet Inspection (DPI) Works (Domain Blocking)
--------------------------------------------------
When the client attempts to load an HTTPS website, their browser sends a standard `CONNECT facebook.com:443 HTTP/1.1` header string initially unencrypted so the proxy knows where to route them. Our Dart server intercepts this exact HTTP string stream, rips out the domain characters, and verifies it against your local security set (`SecurityRulesManager`). If there is a match, the `clientSocket.destroy()` directive is instantly called, killing the connection fundamentally before the TLS/SSL encrypted handshake can even begin!
